What Is Lawn Restoration and How Does It Differ From Lawn Care?

Lawn restoration is the process of rebuilding a damaged or declining lawn through soil correction, aeration, topdressing, overseeding, and sod replacement — not weekly mowing.

Lawn restoration repairs underlying soil and turf health; lawn care maintains an already-healthy lawn through mowing and trimming.

Homeowners across The Villages (a master-planned retirement community of 80,000+ homes spanning Sumter, Lake, and Marion counties along US-301 and FL-44) often confuse the two services. Oxford Lawn focuses solely on the renovation side. That includes core aeration (mechanically pulling soil plugs to relieve compaction), soil amendment, and topdressing (applying a thin layer of compost or sand to level the surface and feed the soil).

Lawn restoration vs lawn maintenance: Restoration is a project — a defined start and finish that fixes a problem, because the soil and root system need rebuilding. Maintenance is ongoing weekly work, because grass keeps growing. The two services complement each other but require different equipment, licensing, and expertise.

Should I Fertilize My Lawn in October in Florida?

Fertilizing your lawn in October in Florida is generally a smart move, since cooler temperatures and the end of the summer fertilizer blackout make it the ideal window for nitrogen and potassium applications.

Yes — October is one of the two best fertilizing months in Central Florida, after the summer rainy-season blackout ends.

October fertilizing helps St. Augustine grass build root reserves before winter dormancy. Many Florida counties enforce a summer fertilizer ban from June 1 to September 30 to prevent nutrient runoff into waterways. By October, that ban lifts, and turf is ready to absorb a balanced application. Sumter County follows Florida statute 403.9337 (the Florida-Friendly Fertilizer Use on Urban Landscapes Act — see flsenate.gov).

What Fertilizer Ratio Works Best in October?

Look for a 15-0-15 or 8-0-16 blend with at least 50% slow-release nitrogen. Phosphorus is restricted in most Florida lawns unless a soil test proves a deficiency.

How Do You Restore St. Augustine Grass and Amend Sandy Soil?

Restoring St. Augustine grass starts with a soil test, followed by pH correction, core aeration, compost topdressing, and targeted plug or sod replacement in bare areas.

Test soil first, then aerate, amend, topdress, and replace dead patches with matching sod.

Soil amendment for St. Augustine grass in The Villages typically requires raising organic matter from the native 1-2% up to 4-5%. Sandy Florida soils don't hold nutrients or moisture well, so a ¼-inch compost topdressing makes a major difference. Experts at Oxford Lawn recommend pairing topdressing with humic acid and a light wetting-agent application, especially on west-facing lawns near the Polo Field or along Buena Vista Boulevard, where afternoon sun is most intense.
