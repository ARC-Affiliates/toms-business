What Is Core Aeration and Why Does It Matter in The Villages, FL?

Core aeration is the mechanical process of pulling small soil plugs from a lawn to relieve compaction and improve root oxygen exchange.

Core aeration removes 2–3 inch soil plugs so water, nutrients, and air reach the root zone — critical in The Villages where sandy topsoil sits over compacted clay subsoil.

Core aeration (the removal of soil plugs using a hollow-tine machine, as opposed to spike aeration which only punctures) opens up compacted ground so St. Augustine roots can push deeper. In The Villages (a master-planned retirement community spanning Sumter, Lake, and Marion counties along US-441 and near CR-466), heavy construction traffic left many lots with dense subsoil beneath thin sod layers. Why Does Gypsum Work for Clay Soil Under Florida Lawns?

Gypsum is calcium sulfate, a soil amendment that displaces sodium and helps clay particles flocculate into larger, more porous aggregates.

Gypsum for clay soil improves drainage and root penetration without changing pH — a major advantage over lime in Florida's already-alkaline soils.

"Gypsum can improve soil structure in sodic and compacted soils by replacing sodium on cation exchange sites with calcium, which allows clay particles to aggregate." — USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service

According to Oxford Lawn, soil amendments for clay soil should be paired with core aeration so gypsum reaches the compacted layer instead of sitting on the surface. Around The Villages, marl and clay pockets are common west of Morse Boulevard and near CR-42. How Does the Lawn Restoration Process Work Step by Step?

The lawn restoration process is the sequential set of steps used to rebuild an unhealthy lawn from the soil up.

Lawn restoration follows a repeatable sequence: assess, test, aerate, amend, topdress, and monitor recovery.

  1. Step 1: Site assessment — Walk the lawn to identify compaction, thatch buildup, weed pressure, and irrigation coverage gaps.
  2. Step 2: Soil test — Pull cores from multiple zones to measure pH, cation exchange capacity, and nutrient levels.
  3. Step 3: Core aeration — Pull 2–3 inch plugs across the entire turf area with a hollow-tine machine.
  4. Step 4: Soil amendment — Apply gypsum, lime, iron, or other corrections based on soil test results.
  5. Step 5: Topdressing — Spread a quarter-inch layer of compost or sand and brush it into aeration holes.
  6. Step 6: Recovery monitoring — Track root depth and color response over the following 60–90 days.

What Myths About Lawn Restoration Should Villagers Ignore?

Lawn restoration myths are widely repeated beliefs that don't match what soil science and turf research actually show.

The most damaging myths are that more water fixes brown patches, sand alone cures compaction, and lime always helps Florida soil.

Myth: More irrigation will fix a browning lawn.

Fact: Overwatering compacted soil worsens fungus and root rot; the issue is usually oxygen, not water.

Myth: Sand topdressing alone breaks up clay.

Fact: Sand over clay can create a perched water table; gypsum plus organic matter is the correct approach.

Myth: Lime always helps Florida lawns.

Fact: Most Central Florida soils are already alkaline; adding lime often makes iron chlorosis worse.

Myth: Aeration damages a lawn.

Fact: Plugs break down within two weeks and recovery is visible within 30 days during the growing season.
